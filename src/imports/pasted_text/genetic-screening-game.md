Scene 1 of 5. The clinic
Body text
You and your partner have been trying to conceive for two years, and after fertility testing, your specialist has recommended IVF.
At the first consultation, the clinic offers pre-implantation genetic testing (PGT). They can screen the embryos for chromosomal abnormalities before transfer, which improves the success rate and reduces the risk of miscarriage. It costs $2,500, adds ten days to the process, and isn’t covered by Medicare.
Some couples find the extra information reassuring. Others prefer not to know.
You and your partner have been trying to conceive for two years, and after fertility testing, your specialist has recommended IVF.
At the first consultation, the clinic offers preimplantation genetic testing (PGT). It can screen embryos for chromosomal abnormalities before transfer, which may improve the chance of a successful pregnancy and reduce the risk of miscarriage.
The counsellor explains that testing isn’t perfect. Some embryos flagged as abnormal may still result in healthy births, and screening can reduce the number of embryos available for transfer. In some cases, no embryos are considered suitable after testing.
The process adds around ten days and costs $2,500 out of pocket, on top of an already expensive IVF cycle. Because it isn’t covered by Medicare, not all couples choose to include it.
Some couples find the extra information reassuring. Others feel it introduces more uncertainty and difficult decisions.

Scene 2 of 5. How far do you screen?
Variant 1. Shown if player chose A in Scene 1 (tested embryos)
Body text
Your PGT results come back. Two embryos are chromosomally normal, and the clinic is ready to schedule the transfer.
Before you hang up, the counsellor mentions the clinic’s expanded panel. For an extra $3,000, it screens for single-gene diseases like cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, and Tay-Sachs, and provides polygenic risk scores for conditions like diabetes, heart disease, and some cancers. It also includes predicted scores for traits like height and cognitive ability, although these are less reliable than the disease markers.
She adds a caveat. Polygenic scores are most reliable for people of European ancestry, because the research datasets skew that way. The scores still carry meaning for other backgrounds, but with wider error margins.
“Most people don’t carry anything serious,” she says, “but if you do, it’s better to know now.”

Variant 2. Shown if player chose A in Scene 2 (disease panel only)
Body text
The counsellor calls with the disease panel results. One of your embryos stopped developing before reaching the transfer stage. It happens, even with chromosomally normal embryos. You have one remaining.
That remaining embryo is clear on the named disease conditions. No cystic fibrosis, no sickle cell, no Tay-Sachs, no Huntington’s.
But it has a variant of uncertain significance in a gene associated with a hereditary cancer syndrome. A VUS, in clinical shorthand. The variant hasn’t been well-characterised in the population. It might slightly increase lifetime cancer risk. It might do nothing at all. The research hasn’t caught up, and clearer answers could be several years away.


You can implant this embryo and accept the uncertainty. You can also start another IVF cycle and hope for a cleaner result. Another cycle takes months, costs thousands, and offers no guarantee.

Variant 2. Shown if player chose B in Scene 4 (kept private)
It’s 2045 and your child is 18. Genetic screening remained in the private market. The technology has become cheaper and more sophisticated, but access still tracks closely with income. Rates of serious inherited conditions have fallen among wealthier families and stayed roughly flat in lower-income communities. Researchers have a name for it now: the genomic divide.
Shared body text
A new debate has emerged. Public health modelling suggests that near-universal embryo screening could eliminate several serious genetic conditions within two generations, and some ethicists argue that if the technology is safe and effective, failing to use it causes preventable suffering.
A private member’s bill has been introduced proposing mandatory genetic screening for all IVF pregnancies, with transfer of affected embryos prohibited in cases of severe, early-onset conditions.
Disability rights groups have condemned the proposal as eugenics. They argue that “severe” is being defined by people who don’t live with these conditions, and that the community of people with them has not been consulted. Some parents who chose not to screen, and now have children with the conditions this bill would eliminate, feel their families are being told they shouldn’t exist.
Others, including some parents of children with severe genetic conditions, support the bill. They argue that if a technology can prevent suffering, there is an obligation to use it. A few note that “voluntary” has never really meant voluntary. It has meant voluntary for people who can afford it, and routine for people who cannot refuse without seeming negligent.
Your adult child asks where you stand. They’re studying bioethics at university and writing an essay on reproductive autonomy.
